What is filler?

Filler is one of many games offered by the Apple iMessage app: GamePigeon. Considered a simpler chess by many (or at least 1), the goal of Filler is to collect as many squares as possible before all squares have been claimed. You do so by changing your color to the color of a neighboring square. The catch: you cannot change to your opponent's color.

Emerging Strategies

Is it best to try to cut your opponent off from a corner of the board, claiming 9 squares for the price of 3? Is it better to try to reach the center as quickly as possible to expand as quickly as possible in the late game? Should you claim 1 square this turn instead of 2, to force your  opponent to claim 1 square instead of 3? With such an evolving meta surrounding the mind game, nay mind battle, that is Filler, there is a new and increasing demand for a tool for competitors to use to hone their Filler skills.

The Algorithm

To evaluate a board state, we run a recursive minimax algorithm with alpha-beta pruning optimization. It examines each board state a set number of moves in the future and calculates the relative value of that board for each player. It then finds the best move at each level for each player, and returns what the best move would be for the current board, if each player makes their best move for 14 turns straight. Alpha-beta pruning allows the algorithm to disregard moves that are clearly inferior to moves that have been already calculated.

Maximum Efficiency

During beta, we analyzed several different implementations to see which took the least amount of time to calculate the optimal board.

Above you can see various graphs showing the calculation time required for 5 different test boards. The left two graphs compare the difference in time for calculating the best board in transpiled web assembly versus in Rust. The leftmost is normalized by the number of boards examined before making a conclusion. The significant overlap between all various versions (represented in brown and green in the first and second graphs respectively) show that the difference between the 2 single-threaded implementations is not statistically sagnificant.

The third graph examines the difference in time between our multithreaded Rust implementation and our multithreaded WASM implementation, finding the Rust implementation to be much faster, albeit less available.

The final graph examines the difference in time between our distributed, multithreaded WASM implementation and normal multithreaded WASM implementation with a significant overlap. This shows that the difference between a distributed and single-machine computation can vary across different board varieties, but ultimately is rather insignificant.
